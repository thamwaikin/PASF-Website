About

The Pan-Asian Student Forum was first held in Beijing in 2013 and involved Peking University (Yuanpei College), Seoul National University (College of Liberal Studies) and the Chinese University of Hong Kong (S. H. Ho College).

The University of Tokyo (College of Arts & Sciences) joined the second iteration of the forum held in Seoul in 2014. NUS (USP) was added to the consortium in 2015.

The forum was inaugurated to provide a platform for students from distinguished universities in Asia to meet regularly and share views on topics that impact countries in the region and around the world.

Theme

Our Identity in a Changing World

In this modern and increasingly connected world, we are exposed to and influenced by many different cultures, schools of thought and people. With the advent of technology, old boundaries are blurred. As we come to embrace the ideals, values and even people of other cultures and communities, our identity adapts to this new environment. Yet is this new identity the same as the one of yesteryear? If this continues, will the world eventually become one homogenous society?

What gives “People” their identity?

As lines start to blur in this globalised world and with the influx and outflow of people across borders, what defines a “People”? What is the difference between an ethnic and communal identity? If you are ethnically Korean, but born and raised in America, are you Korean? Are you American? Would an ethnically British person who grew up in Korea be seen as Korean? Is the concept of nations an outdated one? In the European Union, the Open Border Policy is already blurring the lines between various European nations. Will we then grow to see ourselves as part of a region? If you ask yourself whether you are, for example, Singaporean or Asian, which would you answer? What factors and thoughts are involved in this choice? What defines this identity?

Who defines our society’s cultural identity?

Culture is an amorphous thing. It’s hard to capture in words and means different things to different people. It is interacted with and responded to differently. What youths consider a quintessential part of our society’s culture may differ from the older generation. So who is in charge of the trajectory of culture? Although the older generation may know society’s culture “the best”, should the youth not have the right to define ‘culture’ as future leaders of society? How do we take ownership in shaping the trajectory of culture? Do we sit passively and wait for culture to change? Do we respond to it? Or do we go a step further and actively try to shape it? And, before we address all these questions, how is culture created? How are we socialised? Where do our thoughts come from? Our family? Our community? Our education?

Close

What identities do spaces take in our society?

Many of our societies often have structures and spaces that are culturally and historically rich. Some of these spaces possess a strong sense of symbolism, while other serve as historic memory or architectural icons. Some of these structures are preserved as a reminder of their once great glory, while others are restored and repurposed, converting an old historical building into a museum or a retail space. Others are simply demolished in order to make way for modern developments. Should we preserve these structures in their natural state, like the Coliseum, Rome, or should we restore them to their former glory, like Himeji Castle, Japan? Should we allow for the repurposing of these spaces, converting historical streets into shopping areas for tourism or into museums for historical memory? When repurposing these spaces can we evolve them to a new purpose, like turning an old building into a culmination of art studios? Are we defilling the identity of these spaces by doing so? How do we even construe whether a space or structure is important or not? Should the government be responsible for protecting and restoring these spaces?
